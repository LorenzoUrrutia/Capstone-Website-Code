Common Dyslexia Profiles

Dyslexia can appear in different ways, but the shared pattern is that reading, spelling, and word recognition require more effort than expected for the person's age and learning opportunities.

  • Phonological processing difficulties: Trouble identifying and manipulating sounds in words, which affects decoding and spelling.
  • Reading fluency difficulties: Slow, effortful reading that may sound choppy or lack accuracy.
  • Spelling and written expression difficulties: Persistent spelling errors and difficulty organizing written language.
  • Word retrieval difficulties: Trouble quickly recalling words, especially when reading aloud or writing.

Common presentations

  • Mild: Reading is slower and requires extra effort, but basic decoding is possible with support.
  • Moderate: Significant difficulty with decoding, spelling, and fluency that interferes with academic tasks.
  • Severe: Reading and spelling are extremely challenging without intensive, specialized instruction.

Common Symptoms for Kids

  • Difficulty spelling simple words
  • Trouble learning the names of letters
  • Difficulty telling apart letters with similar shapes: Mixing up "d" and "b" or "p" and "q"
  • Trouble Rhyming
  • Difficulty sounding out new words
  • Trouble associating sounds with letters or parts of words
  • Trouble learning how sounds go together
  • Mixing up the position of sounds in a word

Symptoms for Adults

  • Making a lot of mistakes when reading
  • Avoiding reading whenever possible
  • Difficulty with spelling and writing
  • Trouble summarizing a story
  • Difficulty with word retrieval
