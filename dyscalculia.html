What is Dyscalculia?

Dyscalculia is a learning disorder that primarily impairs number sense, quantity understanding, and the ability to perform and reason about mathematical operations.

It affects approximately 3 to 7 percent of the population worldwide and is typically identified in early elementary school, when formal math instruction begins, though challenges may become more apparent as math demands increase.

Dyscalculia Profiles

Dyscalculia exists on a spectrum, so people experience different levels and types of math-related difficulty.

Common symptoms include weak number sense, trouble estimating quantities, and difficulty with basic arithmetic facts and multi-step calculations. The experience varies by individual.

Causes

The exact cause of dyscalculia is not fully understood, but research points to neurological and genetic factors.

Brain development
Dyscalculia is linked to differences in how the brain develops systems for understanding numbers. Certain brain areas involved in number processing may function differently, making basic number sense and arithmetic more challenging.
Genetics
Dyscalculia can run in families, suggesting a genetic component that affects how numerical abilities develop in the brain.

Diagnosis

Educational professionals and parents are often the first to notice dyscalculia in a child. Screeners or classroom assessments can flag risk, but a comprehensive evaluation is needed for a formal diagnosis.

The two main criteria are persistent difficulty mastering number sense and difficulty with mathematical reasoning.
