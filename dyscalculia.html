What is Dyscalculia?

Dyscalculia is a learning disorder that primarily impairs number sense, quantity understanding, and the ability to perform and reason about mathematical operations.
Dyscalculia affects approximately 3 to 7 percent of the population worldwide and is typically identified in early elementary school, when formal math instruction begins, though challenges may become more apparent later as math demands increase.

Dyscalculia Profiles

Dyscalculia exists on a spectrum, so people experience different levels and types of math-related difficulty
Common symptoms include weak number sense, trouble estimating quantities, and difficulty with basic arithmetic facts and multi-step calculations

Causes

Dyscalculia can sometimes run in families, suggesting there may be a genetic component that affects how numerical abilities develop.
It is also linked to differences in how the brain develops systems for understanding numbers, which can make it harder to judge numerical size and perform calculations.
Research shows that certain brain areas involved in number processing may function differently in individuals with dyscalculia.
One important area helps with understanding quantity and comparing numbers, so when it works differently, basic number sense and arithmetic can be more challenging.

Diagnosis

Educational proffesionals and parents are the most likely to first notice Dyscalculia in a child. Screeners or classroom assessments can flag risk, but a comprehensive evaluation is needed for a diagnosis
The two main types of criteria to diagnose a student with Dyscalculia are difficulty mastering number sense and difficulty with mathimatical reasoning
