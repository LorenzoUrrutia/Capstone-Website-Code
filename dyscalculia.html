What is Dyscalculia?

  • Dyscalculia is a learning disorder that primarily impairs number sense, quantity understanding, and the ability to perform and reason about mathematical operations.
  • Dyscalculia affects approximately 3 to 7 percent of the population worldwide and is typically identified in early elementary school, when formal math instruction begins, though challenges may become more apparent later as math demands increase.

Dyscalculia Profiles

  • Dyscalculia exists on a spectrum, so people experience different levels and types of math-related difficulty
  • Common symptoms include weak number sense, trouble estimating quantities, and difficulty with basic arithmetic facts and multi-step calculations

Causes

  • There is evidence that dyscalculia can have a genetic component and may run in families. Studies suggest that differences in how certain brain networks involved in number processing develop may contribute to difficulties understanding numerical magnitude and performing calculations.
  • Neuroimaging research has found differences in activity in areas of the brain associated with numerical cognition, particularly the intraparietal sulcus within the parietal lobe. This region plays a key role in representing numerical quantity and comparing numbers, which can make basic number sense and arithmetic more challenging for individuals with dyscalculia.

Diagnosis

  • Educational proffesionals and parents are the most likely to first notice Dyscalculia in a child
  • Screeners or classroom assessments can flag risk, but a comprehensive evaluation is needed for a diagnosis
  • The two main types of criteria to diagnose a student with Dyscalculia are difficulty mastering number sense and difficulty with mathimatical reasoning
