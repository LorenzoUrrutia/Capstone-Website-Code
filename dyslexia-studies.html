Study

A study compared 10 children with dyslexia to age-matched and reading-matched control groups, using MRI and behavioral tests to identify brain differences specifically linked to dyslexia rather than just lower reading levels

Study details

  • Behavioral Benchmarking: Before the scans, children took a battery of tests like Word Attack (reading fake words) and the PPVT (vocabulary) to map out their exact reading and language levels.
  • The In-Scanner Task: Participants then entered an fMRI scanner to perform a Word-Rhyme Task, where they had to decide if words like "bait" and "gate" rhymed while the machine tracked their brain activity.
  • Structural Comparisons: The team took detailed anatomical scans to measure Gray Matter Volume, confirming that any differences they found were based on how the brain functions, not its physical size or shape.
  • Quality Control: During the data analysis, they used software to account for head motion and scaled the signals to ensure the results weren't skewed by a child wiggling in the scanner.
  • Statistical Analysis: Finally, they compared the brain maps across all three groups using Random-Effects Models to isolate the specific regions where dyslexic brains worked differently than both older and younger typical readers.

Results

  • Left Temporoparietal Cortex: Found to be underactive in dyslexic children, impacting their ability to map letters to sounds.
  • Left Inferior Frontal Gyrus: Showed reduced activity, suggesting difficulties with the "inner speech" needed for phonological processing.
  • Occipitotemporal Cortex: Identified as less active in dyslexics, which affects their ability to quickly recognize word shapes.
