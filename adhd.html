What is ADHD?

ADHD stands for Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. It is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects 5 to 11 percent of people in the United States. It impacts how the brain functions, particularly in areas related to attention, impulse control, and hyperactivity.

People with ADHD have trouble with a group of key skills known as executive function. That creates challenges in many areas of life, from school to work to everyday living.

Causes of ADHD

ADHD's exact cause is not fully understood, but strong evidence shows it is primarily influenced by genetic and neurodevelopmental factors.

Genetics
ADHD is highly heritable and often runs in families. Many genes each contribute small effects to how attention and impulse control develop.
Brain development & chemistry
Differences in brain networks involved in attention and self-regulation, along with neurotransmitters like dopamine and norepinephrine, affect focus, motivation, and impulse control.
Early-life risk factors
Factors such as premature birth, low birth weight, or prenatal exposure to substances like lead can increase risk, but they do not cause ADHD on their own.

Diagnosis of ADHD

ADHD is diagnosed by a trained healthcare professional, such as a pediatrician, psychologist, or psychiatrist. There is no single test for ADHD; diagnosis is based on patterns of behavior over time.

Clinicians gather information from multiple sources, including interviews with the individual and caregivers, teacher reports or school records, and a review of medical, developmental, and educational history.
