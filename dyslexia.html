Dyslexia

What is Dyslexia?

Dyslexia is a lifelong reading-related disorder that makes reading and language-related tasks harder. It primarily impacts a person's phonological processing: the ability to accurately and fluently recognize words, decode unfamiliar words, and spell.

Dyslexia affects 7% of the population worldwide and occurs because of disruptions in the way the brain processes written and spoken language.

Dyslexia Profiles

Dyslexia does not have official subtypes. Instead, it exists on a spectrum, meaning individuals can experience different challenges and levels of difficulty with reading and language.

Some symptoms of dyslexia include difficulty with accurate and fluent word recognition, poor spelling, and weak decoding abilities. The experience varies widely from person to person.

Causes of Dyslexia

Dyslexia is a neurodevelopmental disorder caused by differences in how the brain develops and functions.

Brain development
Brain-imaging studies show that kids with dyslexia have less activity in areas that process language. These neurological differences affect how the brain handles phonological information.
Genetics
Dyslexia runs in families. Researchers have identified several genes that affect brain development and language skills, making it highly heritable.

Diagnosis

Dyslexia is not diagnosed with a single test. Initial assessments involve short dyslexia screenings to flag a risk. Diagnosis is typically made when there is a significant gap between listening comprehension skills and word reading and spelling skills.
