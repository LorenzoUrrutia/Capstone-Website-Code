Dyscalculia Symptoms

Dyscalculia can affect how a person understands quantities, remembers math facts, and works through number-based tasks. The signs can look different at different ages, but they usually reflect difficulty making numbers feel stable and meaningful.

What dyscalculia affects

  • Visual processingthe ability of your eyes to take in the whole equation and send each part to the brain to sort out.
  • Short-term memorycapacity to hold numbers, symbols, and their order in mind while solving.
  • Language processingability to translate symbols like +, =, and ? into their meanings in the problem.
  • Long-term memoryability to recall the steps for solving a familiar operation (like addition).
  • Number senseability to connect a numeral such as “2” to the idea of a specific quantity.
  • Calculationall of these pieces combine to work out the answer (e.g., 2 + 2 = 4).

Symptoms by age

The symptoms of dyscalculia depend on which parts of the process a person struggles with most and the person's age.

Young children

  • Counting upward.
  • Connecting a number to that many of an object: number 7 to that many blocks in front of them.
  • Recognizing numbers and math symbols.
  • Organizing numbers
  • Recognizing and using number lines.
  • Learning using money

School age children

  • Counting on fingers with small numbers when not necessary.
  • Identifying small quantities of items just by looking
  • Doing simple calculations from memory.
  • Memorizing multiplication tables.
  • Recognizing the same math problem when the order of the numbers or symbols changes.
  • Understanding word problems or more advanced symbols like "greater then" or "less than".
  • Organizing numbers by scale (10s, 100s, 1,000s) or decimal place (0.1, 0.01, 0.001).

Teenagers and adults

  • Counting backward.
  • Solving word problems.
  • Breaking down problems into multiple steps to solve them.
  • Measuring items.
  • Measuring quantities (such as for cooking/baking recipes).
  • Using money to pay for items, exchanging bills for coins, and making change.
  • Understanding and converting fractions.
